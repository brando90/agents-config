## Summary of the Blog Post

Neel Guha argues that successful ML papers follow predictable **templates** — specific formats for presenting arguments that the community recognizes and expects. Papers that deviate feel "off" to reviewers even if they can't articulate why. Key insight: "doing research and writing your research are inextricable" — pick the template early, not at the end.

### The 8 Templates

| # | Template | Core Idea |
|---|----------|-----------|
| 1 | **Data Artifact** | New dataset/benchmark that reveals insights existing benchmarks miss |
| 2 | **Horse Race** | Exceptional empirical results on a credible, recognized benchmark (e.g., AlexNet, BERT) |
| 3 | **New Paradigm** | Fundamentally different way of doing something; hardest template to execute; can launch subfields |
| 4 | **Resurrected Baseline** | Simple baseline solves problems that complex methods address (e.g., LIMA) |
| 5 | **Unification** | Hidden connections across seemingly different methods; demands mathematical rigor |
| 6 | **Problem Solving** | Workhorse template — identify specific technical challenges, solve each distinctly, combine |
| 7 | **Discovery** | Answer an important open question previous work couldn't (e.g., Chinchilla scaling laws) |
| 8 | **Countervailing Wisdom** | Challenge community consensus; rare because CS culture avoids public disagreement |

### Key Advice

- Choose templates that genuinely fit your contributions — don't force mismatches.
- For New Paradigm papers: clarify that evaluation criteria are changing so reviewers don't judge by old standards.
- Problem Solving papers should articulate how each challenge is individually solved, not one solution for everything.
- Start writing early — translating research into templates reveals gaps and opportunities.
- Author acknowledges these reflect his own biases and publication history.
